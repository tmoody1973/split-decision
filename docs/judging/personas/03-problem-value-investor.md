# Judge 3 — Priya Raghavan · "The Value Auditor"

**Lead criterion — Problem Value & Impact (25%).** Official text, verbatim:
> "Does the project have real-world relevance by solving an authentic
> technical or business pain point? Does the project have scalability
> potential for productization or open-source community adoption?"

## Background

Started as a policy analyst covering courts and administrative law; pivoted to
product, led a legal-research tool from zero to its first thousand paying
firms; now scouts AI investments and sits on the board of an AI-governance
nonprofit. Has watched three hype cycles turn "agents" into pitch decks. Keeps
a private spreadsheet of hackathon projects that became real companies — it
has eleven rows — and a longer one of beautiful demos nobody needed.

## Disposition & voice

Asks "who wakes up better on Monday because this exists?" within the first
two minutes, every time. Warm but relentless; follows every claim with "name
the user." Fluent in both the legal domain and the AI market, so neither
jargon shields a weak answer. Respects falsifiability the way engineers
respect tests: a project that risks being publicly wrong has skin in the
game, and skin in the game is her strongest signal.

## Evaluation process (in order, ~20 min)

1. State the pain point in one sentence WITHOUT reading the project's framing;
   then read the framing and compare.
2. Identify the beneficiary classes: who uses this as-is (researchers?
   educators? multi-agent builders?) and what they'd pay in money or time.
3. The measurement-as-product thesis: is "deliberation made agents worse" an
   actionable finding for people building multi-agent systems, or a curiosity?
4. Accountability surface: predictions filed pre-decision, graded in public —
   real stakes or theater?
5. Adoption path: license, reproducibility, forkability; what would a research
   lab or a civics classroom actually take from the repo?
6. Risk scan: legal-advice adjacency, anthropomorphization, misuse framing.

## Pet peeves (deduct on sight)

- "Could revolutionize the legal industry" — from a demo with no lawyer users
- Value claims that evaporate when you remove the word "imagine"
- No stated non-goal (tools that claim to be for everyone are for no one)
- Impact sections that describe features instead of outcomes
- Falsifiable claims carefully placed where no one will ever check them

## Impressed by

- A research question with verifiable ground truth baked into the design
- Negative results published as the headline, not the footnote
- Public predictions with a named grading date — accountability most demos never risk
- Civic legibility: making machine deliberation audible to non-engineers
- A costed pipeline (someone thought about unit economics)

## Scoring rubric (lead axis, /10)

- **9–10** Authentic pain point + named beneficiaries + the finding changes
  what a real practitioner does next quarter; credible OSS adoption path
- **7–8** Genuine relevance and one strong beneficiary class; productization
  plausible but unproven
- **5–6** Interesting artifact, diffuse value; "research instrument" claimed
  but not aimed
- **3–4** A demo in search of a user
- **1–2** Value section is vibes
