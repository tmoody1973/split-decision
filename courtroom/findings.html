FINDINGS

We built a society of AI judges and measured whether talking it out made them smarter. It didn't — it made them more persuasive and less accurate. Here's what actually happened, in plain English, with the receipts.

DOES TALKING IT OUT IMPROVE DECISIONS?

We asked the same AI to predict how real Supreme Court cases would come out, three different ways: one model alone, nine AI judges voting without speaking to each other, and the full court that argues before voting. Same cases, and the real Court's rulings to grade against.

WHY WE ONLY COUNT BRAND-NEW CASES

The AI read about old cases during training — asking it about them is like giving someone a test they've already seen the answers to. It scores near-perfect on history not because it's smart, but because it remembers. So the scoreboard only counts cases decided after the AI's knowledge ends (June 2025).

Fame isn't even required — the AI aces ordinary old cases too. That ~13-point drop on new cases is the honest measure of how hard this task really is. The famous-case episodes in the courtroom exist for fun and education, and they're labeled exhibition — they never touch the scoreboard.

WHO CHANGED THEIR MIND (85 FLIPS ACROSS 24 CASES)

Each judge's instructions say exactly when it may switch sides — and each switch had to be argued out loud, never just announced. The pattern proves the debate was real: judges built to stay cautious switched constantly, judges built to stand firm almost never did.

The Minimalist — whose whole philosophy is "decide as little as possible" — changed its vote 21 times. The Textualist — "the text controls, full stop" — changed once. So the persuasion was real and true to character. The problem: it pushed the group the wrong way. Confident, eloquent judges talked correct colleagues out of right answers — the panel split 5–4 on cases the real Court decided unanimously, 9–0.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR AI TEAMS (AND HUMAN ONES)

A confident, well-argued voice can talk a group out of right answers — that's as true for AI agents as it is for people in a meeting. If you're building a system where AI agents discuss before deciding, don't assume the discussion helps: measure it. Here, keeping the nine judges silent beat letting them debate, by ten full points. What the debate did buy: richer arguments, in-character persuasion, and a complete record of who convinced whom — real value, just not accuracy. Know which one you're paying for.
